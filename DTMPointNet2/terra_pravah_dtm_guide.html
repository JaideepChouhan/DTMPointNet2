Chapter 01

What Does Raw LiDAR Actually Look Like?

Imagine standing at the edge of a village in Rajasthan at dusk. The Physical Survey of India aircraft passes overhead at 600 metres, firing 500,000 laser pulses per second at the earth below. Every pulse that bounces back is recorded as a point in three-dimensional space — a coordinate and a time-of-flight.

By the time the aircraft lands, it has captured somewhere between 50 and 200 points per square metre of that village. That is extraordinary resolution — far finer than any satellite image. But here is the problem: the laser does not know what it hits. It returns a point for the ground, yes — but also one for every mango leaf, every cattle horn, every thatched rooftop, every electricity pole.

For Terra Pravah, this is catastrophic. Our drainage network engine needs to know where water flows. Water flows on ground. A rooftop point left in the terrain model becomes a false ridge that blocks every simulated raindrop. A haystack becomes a phantom hill that creates a ghost catchment boundary.

The core challenge, stated simply:
Given a cloud of 2.4 million 3D points where 84% are NOT ground, correctly label every single one — including kuccha mud walls (height 1.2–2.5 m), sleeping cattle (~1.0 m), courtyard slabs (just 5–20 cm above ground), and haystacks (0.3–1.5 m above ground) — with >95% accuracy.

Chapter 02

The Journey to a Clean DTM

A Digital Terrain Model is not just a grid of elevation values. For it to be useful to Terra Pravah's hydraulic engine, it must satisfy three conditions simultaneously — and none of them can be compromised.

A DTM that contains a single building left standing is not 84% useful — it is entirely wrong. One false ridge can redirect an entire watershed boundary by kilometres.

— Hydrological correctness requirement for drainage design
Three Non-Negotiable Properties
01
Topographic Fidelity
No False Objects
Every non-ground point — trees, buildings, cattle, haystacks — must be removed. The resulting surface must represent only bare earth. Zero tolerance for structural artefacts that would create phantom ridges or false depressions.
02
Hydrological Continuity
No Internal Sinks
After removing buildings and trees, gaps appear in coverage. These gaps must be filled by intelligent interpolation — not just nearest-neighbour. Every cell must drain to a lower neighbour or the domain boundary. No isolated depressions. Priority-Flood conditioning enforces this.
03
Spatial Completeness
No Holes or Voids
IDW interpolation fills structural voids left by building footprint removal. Gaussian smoothing removes residual artefacts without over-smoothing genuine terrain relief. The final raster must have zero NoData pixels within the survey boundary.
Chapter 03

PointNet++ MSG — Why It Works

Most computer vision systems operate on images — regular 2D grids where convolutions slide effortlessly across uniform pixel spacing. LiDAR point clouds are fundamentally different. They are unordered, irregular, and variable-density. A convolutional neural network has no meaningful definition of "next pixel" in a point cloud.

PointNet++ (Qi et al., NeurIPS 2017) solves this with a beautiful idea borrowed from classical signal processing: sample points to serve as centroids, define spherical neighbourhoods around them, aggregate features within each neighbourhood. Multi-Scale Grouping (MSG) does this at multiple radii simultaneously, capturing both fine surface texture and broad terrain context in a single forward pass.

Why MSG Matters for Indian Villages

The two radii in each SA-MSG layer are not arbitrary. r=0.5 m captures the fine-grain surface roughness that distinguishes a smooth thatched roof from rough bare earth. r=1.5 m captures the broader geometry — a wall's vertical face creates a very specific pattern of points in a 1.5 m sphere that no ground surface replicates.

At the SA2 level, r=6.0 m sees across an entire compound — giving the network the contextual information that a patch of points surrounded by ground-level points on all sides is likely to be ground, even if its local geometry is ambiguous.

Chapter 04

From Points to Hydrologically Valid Terrain

Ground classification is step one. But classified points are still just a cloud — they need to become a continuous, gap-free, hydrologically valid raster surface before Terra Pravah can route water across them. This is where the DTM cleaning pipeline earns its keep.

01
AI Inference · dtm_builder.py
PointNet++ Ground Classification
The trained model classifies every point in the full-resolution LAS file. Processing is chunked (50,000 pts at a time) to handle files up to 2 GB on 8 CPU threads. Both best_model.pth and the SWA-averaged model are evaluated; the higher-accuracy one is selected using optimal_threshold.json. Only points labelled ground (class=1) proceed to the next stage.
02
Interpolation · IDW k=12
Structural Void Filling
Where buildings and trees stood, there are now no ground points — just empty space. Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) with k=12 nearest ground neighbours and a power=2 exponent fills these voids. The result is a plausible terrain surface beneath where the structure stood, interpolated from the ground points surrounding it. Resolution: 1.0 m × 1.0 m.
03
Artefact Removal · Gaussian σ=0.5
Residual Noise Smoothing
IDW can leave subtle artefact patterns — star-shaped interpolation rings around isolated ground points, and slight over-smoothing at structural boundaries. A conservative Gaussian filter (σ=0.5) removes these without destroying genuine terrain relief. The filter is applied only to cells flagged as IDW-interpolated, preserving the accuracy of true ground returns.
04
Hydrological Conditioning · Priority-Flood
Pit & Sink Elimination
Even a perfect terrain model contains hydrological sinks — small depressions where D8 flow direction computation cannot find a downslope neighbour. The Priority-Flood algorithm (Wang & Liu 2006) fills every sink by computing the minimum-cost modification to the surface that creates a continuous drainage path to the domain boundary. This step is mandatory before any flow direction or accumulation computation. WhiteboxTools BreachDepressions with breach_dist=5 cells handles flat terrain separately from true sinks.
05
Flow Routing · WhiteboxTools D8
D8 Flow Direction + Accumulation
The conditioned DTM is handed to Terra Pravah's existing WhiteboxTools pipeline. D8 flow direction assigns each cell one of 8 outflow directions. Flow accumulation (specific contributing area) counts upstream cells. At threshold=1000 contributing cells, stream channels are delineated and handed to DrainageAnalysisService for Strahler ordering, Manning's equation sizing, and pipe selection.
06
Export · COG + GPKG
Cloud-Optimised GeoTIFF Output
Two outputs: dtm_conditioned.tif (Cloud-Optimised GeoTIFF with LZW compression, tiled 256×256 for QGIS streaming) and drainage_streams.gpkg (GeoPackage with Strahler order, pipe diameter, Manning's n, and design discharge per segment).
Chapter 05

Integrating Into Terra Pravah

Terra Pravah already has a mature backend: Flask API, WhiteboxTools hydrology, React frontend, Docker deployment, and a working dtm_builder.py service. The AI model does not replace this — it enhances the first step: the LAS → DTM conversion that currently uses classical PMF (Progressive Morphological Filter).

The integration point is surgical: only dtm_builder.py changes. Everything downstream — DrainageAnalysisService, HydrologicalAnalysis, FluidMechanics, the entire React frontend — remains exactly as-is. The AI model's output is the same GeoTIFF format that dtm_builder.py already produces.

backend/services/dtm_builder.py — AI-Enhanced LAS → DTM
# ── NEW: AI ground classification replaces PMF ───────────────────────── class AIGroundClassifier: """Drop-in replacement for classical PMF ground filtering.""" def __init__(self, model_path: str, threshold_path: str): self.device = torch.device("cpu") # i7 inference, no GPU needed torch.set_num_threads(8) # all 8 i7 logical cores # Load trained PointNet++ MSG self.model = DTMPointNet2(in_feat=7).to(self.device) state = torch.load(model_path, map_location="cpu") self.model.load_state_dict(state) self.model.eval() # Load F1-optimal threshold from Kaggle run with open(threshold_path) as f: meta = json.load(f) self.threshold = meta["threshold"] # typically 0.35–0.55 logger.info(f"AI classifier ready | threshold={self.threshold:.2f}") def classify_chunk(self, xyz: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray: """Classify a 4096-point chunk. Returns binary label array.""" feat4 = compute_geospatial_features(xyz) xyz_n = _normalise_xyz(xyz) pts7 = np.concatenate([xyz_n, feat4], axis=1).astype(np.float32) tensor = torch.from_numpy(pts7).unsqueeze(0) # (1, N, 7) with torch.no_grad(): logits = self.model(tensor) # (1, N, 2) probs = F.softmax(logits, dim=-1)[0, :, 1].numpy() return (probs >= self.threshold).astype(np.int8) # 1=ground def classify_las(self, xyz_full: np.ndarray, chunk_size: int = 50_000) -> np.ndarray: """Full-file inference: chunk → classify → stitch.""" labels = np.zeros(len(xyz_full), dtype=np.int8) for i in range(0, len(xyz_full), chunk_size): chunk = xyz_full[i:i+chunk_size] # Sub-sample to 4096 for model input idx = np.random.choice(len(chunk), min(len(chunk), 4096), replace=False) preds = self.classify_chunk(chunk[idx]) # Propagate labels to full chunk via nearest-neighbour labels[i:i+chunk_size] = _propagate_labels(chunk, idx, preds) return labels # ── MODIFIED: DTMBuilder.build_from_las() ───────────────────────────── class DTMBuilder: def build_from_las(self, las_path: str, output_dir: str, use_ai: bool = True) -> str: xyz = _load_las(las_path) # → (N, 3) float32 xyz = _clip_outliers(xyz, pct=(1.0, 99.0)) # remove Z outliers if use_ai and self._ai_classifier: labels = self._ai_classifier.classify_las(xyz) else: labels = _pmf_classify(xyz) # existing fallback ground_xyz = xyz[labels == 1] # only bare earth # IDW interpolation → GeoTIFF (unchanged from original pipeline) dtm_raw = _idw_interpolate(ground_xyz, resolution=1.0, k=12) # Hydrological conditioning (unchanged) dtm_conditioned = _priority_flood(dtm_raw) return _export_cog(dtm_conditioned, output_dir)

Accuracy That Actually Matters

Accuracy numbers on a leaderboard are abstractions. In this domain, accuracy has a physical meaning: every percentage point below 95% represents misclassified points that corrupt the drainage network. At 91%, a typical PMF result, roughly 9% of points are wrong — and those wrong points cluster around exactly the ambiguous structures (kuccha walls, haystacks) where drainage infrastructure decisions are most sensitive.
